What is bioinformatics?

Bioinformatics covers a whole range of biologically-related data science, computational and mathematical study. Given how computational analysis slips into all forms of science these days, it can sometimes be hard to differentiate what counts as bioinformatics.

Over the last several decades, DNA sequencing has exploded as new technologies have been invented and costs have come down significantly. The data analysis and compute power has sometimes struggled to keep up! During this course we are going to mainly focus on the DNA sequencing aspect of bioinformatics, which has undoubtedly become the largest focus globally. Even with this one technology, there are a huge range of varied experiments it can look at including:

What the course will cover

We will be mainly focussing during this course on DNA sequence handling and analysis. Bioinformatics is a broad topic but by the end of the module all participants should be comfortable with handling sequencing data and analysing it in several popular ways, to a standard that would be applicable in many workplaces.

During the course we will begin from how to use and interact with Linux/Unix environment (where bioinformatics is typically performed) and progress through assessing data quality, assembling and annotating sequenced reads, and downstream analysis of these outputs. The teaching will be done through workshops and âlearning by doingâ. You are encouraged to ask questions frequently and help each other along as we work through. Our aim is not just to teach you how to use the software: it is to teach you to understand what the software is doing and how to apply those skills to other software in the future. Bioinformatics is really a Swiss-Army Knife with a huge range of options and no one perfect way of doing things!

What should I do before joining the course?

The vast majority of bioinformatics is performed in a Linux environment, using the Command Line Interface (CLI) which is the traditional way of interacting with an operating system and software that you are running. Most bioinformatics software is not graphical and runs on the command line. The CLI provides a powerful way to access and run system software and is the first thing you will need to get used to. At our first session we will provide you with your unique Linux virtual working machine and begin developing the essential linux skills. However, this is definitely a learning curve that different people take to at different speeds.

Assessment information

Short answer ICA on Mobius

You will have a short-answer ICA (in-class assessment) to complete as you go along. This will be available early on in the course so you can complete it at your own pace. This assessment will check your theoretical knowledge, practical skills and - most importantly of all - your biological interpretation. You will need to do some data analysis to answer the ICA questions; all the files you need will be provided in a clearly-labelled directory on the server. For the assembly data analysis, you will each be assigned a sample labelled with your student number.

The ICA will have the following sections:

  • Sequence pre-processing and quality control

  • Genome assembly (theory)

  • Genome visualization and annotation

  • Phylogenetics

  • Microbial community analysis

  • RNAseq (theory)

  • RNAseq (analysis)

  • Assembly data analysis

  • Assembly data analysis: figures

  • Analysis conclusions

The analysis conclusions sections asks you to interpret the biological meaning of the data you have analysed. Focus on your assigned sample and ask: what does this data mean? What does tell you in the context of the other samples? To reflect the importance of this aspect, the analysis conclusions account for a higher proportion of the assignment marks than any other question. Do not make the mistakes of some former students and skip this question! Almost everyone who failed the ICA last year did so because they did not attempt the analysis conclusions question.

Submission
